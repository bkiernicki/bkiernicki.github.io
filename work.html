Eusociality of Sweat Bees: An Evolutionary Approach

This file contains a powerpoint summary of a scienitfic article entitled "Kinship, parental manipulation and the evoutionary origin of eusociality" (Kapheim et al 2015). It was submitted as a group project for an Animal Behavior course taken at Temple University. The subject in this article is the tropical sweat bee, Megalopta genalis. The main goal of the research within the article was to establish whether eusociality evolved via the daughter's genes (i.e. her willingness to help) or as a result of the mother's genes (maternal manipulation).

Dementia Care Website

Dementia Care is a simulation website created specifically for use by dementia patients and their families. It was part of an individual project for a Texts and Cultures of Science course taken at Temple University. The intention was to extract information from sites used by medical professionals and translate it into laymans terms with a more empathetic approach intended for a broader audience (in particular the patients and family of the patients) without compromising the effectiveness and accuracy of the content.

Science for the Masses or Science for the Classes

The following was an assignment for a Texts and Cultures of Science course taken at Temple University. The goal of the paper was to discuss the ways in which popular science (that which reaches a broad, even non-scientific audience) differs from works that are in fact intended soley for the scientific community. Two articles in particular were chosen. Close reads of each of the articles were performed and word choice was compared and contrasted between the two. Larger implications for each was also theorized based on the given information.

An Analysis of Robert Frost's "Birches"

The following paper is representative of some of my work as an English minor. It contains a brief analysis of Robert Frost's "Birches" conducted for an Interpreting Literature Course taken at Temple University. It focuses on the imagery of the birch trees, the contrasts of the black and white coloration of the trunk, the tendency of the trees to sway back and forth, as well as their larger implications about life itself.
